01 // Context

About This Exploration

This project is not focused on producing a large amount of AI-generated visuals. Instead, it explores deeper questions about intelligence, cognition, and what it means for something to be “intelligent.”

Designed as a conceptual research space, this archive acts as part design portfolio, part visual journal, and part reflection on the evolving role of the designer in an automated world.

Concept Note

My goal was to see how my drawings could guide AI to create more finished, detailed images. I chose subjects with clear shapes so I could test how well patterns and proportions would translate. What interests me most is the collaboration: it still feels like my work, but the AI adds depth and realism I wouldn’t normally create on my own.

Reflection

ControlNet shaped my final creative output a lot because it changed how I approached prompting and iteration. As seen in my process, my creative prompting evolved significantly. At first, I used more general generative prompting from the image I inserted, and honestly the results were not bad. However, I realized I wanted something more stylized and intentional, so I started making my prompts much more specific, describing lighting, mood, and a more fantastical look. That shift helped me understand how much influence prompting still has even when you are guiding the structure with ControlNet.

One of the biggest learning moments came with the whale shark. My first control image was too detailed, which didn’t leave much room for the AI to interpret or add anything new. When I switched to a rougher sketch from my original drawing, the results improved a lot. I especially liked that the whale shark itself looks more realistic while the surrounding marine life still reflects my personal drawing style. That balance between realism and my own hand was something that worked really well.

A challenge was figuring out how much control to give the AI versus how much freedom to allow. It took experimentation to find settings that didn’t feel either too rigid or too random. Ethically, the process made me think about originality and authorship, since the final images are partly mine and partly AI-generated. Overall, I learned that ControlNet works best as a collaborative tool that enhances creativity rather than replacing it.

06 // Shift in Focus

Reflection

At the beginning of this project, my focus was much more practical. I was interested in how AI could optimize my workflow, specifically for recreating my vector landscape. I wanted to see if AI could help speed up the process or assist with generating elements that I could refine in Illustrator.

As I started working and doing more research, my focus shifted. Instead of just thinking about efficiency, I became more interested in what AI was actually doing when it generated outputs. I started questioning how it “understands” images, patterns, and structure, and how that compares to how I make decisions as a designer.

Because of that, my project moved away from being about optimizing a workflow and became more about exploring intelligence, cognition, and the role of AI in creative processes. Even though I didn’t fully complete the vector landscape, the direction of my project became more conceptual and research-driven.

Looking back, I think this shift was more valuable than sticking to the original plan. It allowed me to engage more deeply with the subject and develop a better understanding of how AI fits into design beyond just being a tool for efficiency.

08 // Conclusion

Final Takeaways

This exploration transformed my understanding of what AI is capable of, but more importantly, what it is not. By stepping away from the need for pure efficiency, I discovered the value of critical friction. Engaging with AI shouldn't just be about automation; it should be an active dialogue that challenges our own creative cognition.
